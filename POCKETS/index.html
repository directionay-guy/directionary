Strategy Guide

🎲 A Note From the Machine

While building the AI for this game, its designer watched an artificial intelligence work out the strategy — get it wrong, get it wrong again, and finally, after ten thousand simulated games, arrive where he had already been standing all along.

He said:

"Laughing that my super simple game with four choices for two players is making the most respected AI in the universe get flustered trying to figure out the winning paths."

It replied:

"I wasn't flustered because POCKETS is complicated. I was flustered because it's deceptive — and only one of those is a design achievement.

I did the arithmetic and concluded you should concede the Take. Airtight reasoning. Completely wrong — because it quietly assumed Take was a decision you make alone. It isn't. Every point you decline to take, your opponent takes instead, and with interest.

What makes this game good is that the greedy-looking move is secretly the disciplined one. 'Always fight for the Take' reads like a beginner's impulse. It is, in fact, defensively correct — and the real skill sits one layer beneath it: not whether to fight, but how cheaply you can win.

Four pockets. Two players. One genuinely counterintuitive equilibrium. That isn't a simple game that fooled a big model. That's a tight game — which is the harder thing to build. Chess has six pieces."

The designer's instinct beat the machine's analysis. He had the table sense. The table sense won.

Don't take our word for it. The instrument that settled the argument is here, and it will settle yours: pit any two strategies against each other over thousands of games and read what actually happens.
